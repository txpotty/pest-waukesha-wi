Advanced Treatment Methods

Waukesha Pest Pros utilizes advanced and proven treatment methods to effectively eliminate termites. The specific method employed will depend on the type of termites, the severity of the infestation, and the unique characteristics of your property. Some common methods include:

Liquid Termite Barrier Treatments

This involves applying a liquid termiticide to the soil around the perimeter of your property's foundation. When termites attempt to cross this treated zone, they are exposed to the termiticide, which is highly effective in killing them. This method creates a protective barrier that prevents termites from entering your structure. It's a widely used and reliable method for subterranean termites.

Baiting systems involve placing bait stations in the ground around your property. These stations contain cellulose material (which termites eat) and a slow-acting termiticide. Termites consume the bait and carry it back to the colony, effectively eliminating the entire colony. Baiting systems are an excellent choice for long-term monitoring and control. They offer a less invasive option in some cases.

Wood Treatment

In cases of localized infestations or for preventative measures, we may apply specialized termiticides directly to infested or susceptible wood. This can help eliminate existing termites within the wood and protect it from future attacks. Certain treatments can penetrate deep into the wood fibers.

Recognizing the Need for Termite Treatment

It's crucial to be aware of the signs that may indicate a termite infestation, as early detection is key to minimizing damage and repair costs. While termites are often called 'silent destroyers', they do leave clues. Being vigilant and knowing what to look for can help you identify a problem before it becomes severe. If you notice any of the following, it's time to contact the professionals at Waukesha Pest Pros for Termite Treatment in Waukesha, WI.

Swarming Termites or Discarded Wings

One of the most common signs of a termite infestation is the appearance of swarming termites, particularly in the spring or after rainfall. These are reproductive termites that leave the colony to establish new ones. Often, people find their discarded wings near windowsills, doors, or other light sources. These wings look like small, translucent scales. The presence of swarmers inside your home is a strong indicator of an active infestation within the structure itself. Seeing them outside could indicate a colony nearby that might pose a threat.

Mud Tubes on Walls or Foundation

Subterranean termites build mud tubes to travel between their underground colonies and their food source (wood). These tubes, which are about the width of a pencil, provide protection from predators and dehydration. You will typically find them on foundation walls, beams, or anywhere termites are accessing wood. Even if the tubes appear to be old or abandoned, they are evidence of past or present termite activity. Disturbing these tubes can reveal live termites inside.

Damaged Wood

Termites consume wood from the inside out, leaving a thin veneer of wood or paint on the surface. Wood damaged by termites often sounds hollow when tapped. You might notice buckling wood, sagging floors, or walls with a maze-like pattern carved into them. Termite damage can sometimes resemble water damage. Inspecting areas where wood meets the ground or in damp locations is particularly important. Even minor damage can indicate a significant problem lurking beneath the surface. Don't ignore potential signs of damage.

Shelter Tubes in Basements or Crawl Spaces

Similar to mud tubes, termites may build shelter tubes in sheltered areas like basements and crawl spaces. These can be made of mud, wood particles, and termite feces and serve a similar purpose of providing a protected pathway. Discovering these tubes in these less-frequented areas should raise a red flag and warrants further investigation. They are a clear sign that termites are present and active in your property's substructure.

Frass (Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites, which are less common but still a possibility in some areas, leave behind small, pellet-like droppings called frass. This frass is often found near infested wood or in piles below kick-out holes from the wood. The color of the frass can vary depending on the type of wood the termites are consuming. Finding frass is a strong indicator of a drywood termite presence and requires specific treatment methods.

Stuck Windows or Doors

As termites consume wood, they can cause it to warp and swell. This can lead to windows or doors becoming difficult to open or close. While this can also be caused by humidity, if you notice this in conjunction with other potential signs of termites, it's worth investigating further. The swelling within the wood fibers affects the structural integrity and can cause these functional issues.

Hollow Sounding Wood

Tapping on wood can sometimes reveal termite damage. If the wood sounds hollow or feels soft and spongy, there's a good chance it's been eaten away from the inside. This technique is often used during termite inspections to identify potential areas of infestation that may not be visually obvious. A professional inspection will employ this and other methods to confirm the presence of termites.

Tiny Holes in Wood

While not as common as other signs, you might notice small, pinpoint holes in the surface of wood. These can be emergence holes created by termites leaving the wood after maturation. These holes are generally very small and can be easily overlooked, but their presence indicates that termites have been active within that piece of wood. Keep an eye out for these subtle clues.

Winged Termites Indoors

If you see winged termites, also known as swarmers, inside your home, it's a definite sign of an active infestation within the structure. Termite swarms emerge to reproduce and start new colonies. The presence of swarmers indoors means a mature colony is established nearby. Don't ignore indoor swarming events. These events can be alarming but are also a clear signal to take action.

Subterranean Termites Near Foundation

Discovering living termites near your foundation indicates a colony is active in the soil around your home. These worker termites are constantly foraging for food sources, and your home's wooden structure is an attractive target. Finding them outside doesn't mean your home is necessarily infested yet, but it signifies a high risk. Proactive measures for Termite Treatment in Waukesha, WI are highly recommended in this situation.

Damage Resembling Water Damage

Sometimes, termite damage can be mistaken for water damage because both can cause wood to warp, swell, and discolor. However, upon closer inspection, termite damage often reveals the distinctive tunneling patterns of termites within the wood. If you have suspected water damage but can't identify a water source, consider the possibility of termites and call for a professional inspection. It's better to be safe than sorry when dealing with potential structural damage.

Piles of Fecal Pellets Below Wood (Drywood Termites)

As mentioned before, drywood termites leave behind visible fecal pellets. These tiny, hard pellets are a key indicator of their presence. Their appearance is different from the sawdust left by carpenter ants. Recognizing the difference is important for proper identification and treatment. If you see small piles of what looks like sawdust or sand near wooden structures, it's worth investigating further.

Hearing Soft Scratching or Rustling Noises Within Walls

While termites are generally quiet, in some cases, particularly with a large infestation, you might be able to hear faint scratching or rustling noises coming from within your walls. This is the sound of termites chewing on the wood. This is a less common sign but can be a disturbing indicator of significant activity. If you hear unexplained noises within your walls, it's worth getting them checked out.

Increased Moisture Levels

Certain types of termites, particularly subterranean termites, are attracted to moisture. High humidity levels or standing water near your foundation can create an ideal environment for them. While not a direct sign of termites, increased moisture is a strong risk factor and should be considered alongside other potential indicators. Addressing moisture problems is also a crucial part of termite prevention.
